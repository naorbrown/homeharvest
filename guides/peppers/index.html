Overview & Varieties

Peppers are more compact, more heat-tolerant, and more productive per square foot than tomatoes. They’re also one of the few fruiting crops that produce indoors under grow lights.

One jalapeño plant in a 3-gallon pot can produce 30–40 peppers per season.

Best varieties for home growing:

  • Sweet bell peppers — 0 Scoville. Longest season (75–90 days) but the most versatile kitchen pepper.
  • Banana peppers — 0–500 Scoville. Mild, compact, and great in containers. 65–75 days.
  • Jalapeño — 2,500–8,000 Scoville. Easiest hot pepper, incredibly productive. 65–80 days.
  • Habanero — 100,000–350,000 Scoville. Intense heat, needs a long warm season (90–100 days).
  • Thai peppers — 50,000–100,000 Scoville. Small, prolific, thrives in 2-gallon pots.
Growing Requirements
Light 8+ hours direct sun
Water When top inch of soil is dry
Soil Well-draining, slightly acidic (pH 6.0–6.8)
Temperature 70–90°F (21–32°C)
Planting 2 weeks late in warm soil beats planting early in cold soil.

Peppers won’t germinate below 65°F, and growth stalls below 55°F at night. More drought-tolerant than tomatoes, but consistent moisture gets the best yields.

Step-by-Step Growing Guide
  1. Start seeds indoors 8–10 weeks before last frost. Aim for 80°F soil temp. A heat mat cuts germination from 2–3 weeks down to 7–10 days.
  2. Provide strong light from day one. 14–16 hours under grow lights, or brightest south-facing window. Rotate pots daily.
  3. Pot up when roots fill the container. Move to 4-inch pots at 3–4 sets of true leaves. Don’t bury stems deeply — peppers rot when buried.
  4. Harden off and transplant after soil warms. Wait until nights stay above 55°F and soil hits 65°F. Final container: 3–5 gallon minimum, 18–24 inch spacing.
  5. Stake when fruiting begins. Plants topple under heavy fruit, especially bells. A bamboo stake or short cage prevents this.
  6. Harvest when firm and full color. Green = unripe. Waiting for red/yellow/orange means sweeter flavor and more vitamins, but picking green pushes the plant to produce more fruit.
Pollination Tips

Peppers are self-pollinating — each flower has both male and female parts. Indoors or if flowers drop without fruiting, give pollination a boost:

  • Shake the plant daily. Tap the stem at midday to release pollen inside open flowers.
  • Use a small paintbrush. Dab inside each open flower with a soft brush or cotton swab.
  • Point a fan at the plants. Oscillating airflow improves pollination and strengthens stems.
  • No cross-pollination worries. Hot peppers near sweet ones won’t make this season’s fruit hot.
Common Problems & Solutions
Flower drop (blossoms fall off without forming fruit)
Caused by nights below 55°F, days above 95°F, or inconsistent watering. Be patient — peppers often drop the first flush and fruit heavily on the second.
Aphids
Cluster on new growth and leaf undersides. Blast with water, spray neem oil weekly, or release ladybugs.
Sunscald (white or tan patches on fruit)
Direct intense sun on exposed fruit. Avoid heavy pruning and use shade cloth during peak heat.
Blossom end rot
Inconsistent watering blocks calcium uptake. Water deeply, mulch, and cut back nitrogen fertilizer.
Slow or stunted growth
Almost always cold soil. Peppers stop growing below 60°F. Black plastic mulch warms soil 5–10°F.
Best Spaces for Peppers

Peppers are excellent container crops that adapt to many environments:

Balcony

3–5 gallon containers in full sun. Heat reflected from walls and floors benefits peppers.

Backyard

Raised beds or rows with 18-inch spacing. Black plastic mulch warms the soil for faster growth.

Indoor Setup

Grow lights make year-round pepper production possible. Compact varieties like Thai and jalapeño do best.
