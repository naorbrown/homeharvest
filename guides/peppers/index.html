Peppers

Sweet bells, hot jalapeños, and everything in between. Peppers love containers and warm, sunny spots — one plant produces dozens per season.

Overview & Varieties

Peppers are closely related to tomatoes and share many growing requirements, but they’re more compact, more heat-tolerant, and arguably more productive per square foot. A single jalapeño plant in a 3-gallon container can produce 30–40 peppers over a season. They’re also one of the few fruiting crops that can produce indoors under grow lights.

Pepper heat is measured on the Scoville scale, from 0 (sweet bells) to over 2 million (Carolina Reaper). Here are the best varieties for home growing:

  • Sweet bell peppers — 0 Scoville. The most popular pepper, available in green, red, yellow, and orange (same pepper at different ripeness stages). Needs the longest season (75–90 days) and the most space. “California Wonder” and “King of the North” are reliable producers.
  • Banana peppers — 0–500 Scoville. Mild, sweet, and extremely productive. Great for frying, pickling, and stuffing. Compact plants that do well in containers. 65–75 days to harvest.
  • Jalapeño — 2,500–8,000 Scoville. The essential hot pepper for home growers. Incredibly productive, easy to grow, and versatile in the kitchen. “Early Jalapeño” produces fruit 10 days sooner than standard varieties. 65–80 days.
  • Habanero — 100,000–350,000 Scoville. Intense heat with a fruity, floral flavor underneath. Needs a longer warm season (90–100 days) than milder types. Start indoors early. One plant produces more than most people can use.
  • Thai peppers — 50,000–100,000 Scoville. Small, prolific, and ornamental. Upright fruits in clusters make the plant visually striking. Excellent for stir-fries and curries. Very compact — thrives in 2-gallon containers.

Growing Requirements

Light 8+ hours direct sun
Water When top inch of soil is dry
Soil Well-draining, slightly acidic (pH 6.0–6.8)
Temperature 70–90°F (21–32°C)

Peppers are warm-season crops that refuse to grow in cold soil. They won’t even germinate below 65°F, and growth stalls below 55°F at night. Patience in spring is critical — planting too early in cold soil is worse than planting 2 weeks late in warm soil. Peppers are more drought-tolerant than tomatoes but produce best with consistent moisture.

Step-by-Step Growing Guide

  1. Start seeds indoors 8–10 weeks before last frost. Pepper seeds need warmth to germinate — soil temperature of 80°F is ideal. Without a heat mat, germination can take 2–3 weeks; with one, expect sprouts in 7–10 days. Plant seeds ¼ inch deep in moist seed-starting mix. Hot pepper varieties generally take longer to germinate than sweet types.
  2. Provide strong light from day one. Pepper seedlings stretch and weaken in low light faster than almost any other vegetable. Place under grow lights for 14–16 hours per day, or in the brightest south-facing window you have. Rotate pots daily to prevent leaning.
  3. Pot up when roots fill the container. Move to 4-inch pots when seedlings have 3–4 sets of true leaves. Unlike tomatoes, don’t bury pepper stems deeply — plant at the same depth they were growing. Peppers can develop stem rot when buried too deep.
  4. Harden off and transplant after soil warms. Begin hardening off 7–10 days before your target planting date. Peppers are more sensitive to cold than tomatoes, so wait until nighttime temperatures are consistently above 55°F and soil temperature is at least 65°F. Plant in their final container (3–5 gallon minimum) or garden spot with 18–24 inch spacing.
  5. Stake when fruiting begins. Pepper plants are sturdy when young but can topple under the weight of ripening fruit, especially bell peppers. A simple bamboo stake with a loose tie prevents this. Cages designed for peppers (shorter than tomato cages) also work well.
  6. Harvest when fruit is firm and reaches full color. Green peppers are simply unripe peppers. Leaving fruit on the plant until it turns red, yellow, or orange (depending on variety) results in dramatically sweeter flavor and higher vitamin content. However, picking peppers while green encourages the plant to produce more fruit overall. It’s a tradeoff — more peppers or better peppers.

Pollination Tips

Peppers are self-pollinating — each flower contains both male and female parts. Outdoors, wind and bees handle pollination easily. But if you’re growing indoors or notice flowers dropping without setting fruit, pollination may need a boost:

  • Gently shake the plant. Once a day, give each plant a gentle shake or tap the main stem. This mimics wind and releases pollen within the flowers. Do this when flowers are open, typically midday.
  • Use a small paintbrush. For precision, dab inside each open flower with a soft brush or cotton swab. This transfers pollen to the stigma and almost guarantees fruit set.
  • Point a fan at the plants. A gentle oscillating fan creates airflow that improves pollination and strengthens stems. Run it for a few hours daily.
  • No cross-pollination worries. Despite myths, growing hot and sweet peppers near each other does not make sweet peppers hot (that only affects saved seeds, not the current season’s fruit).

Common Problems & Solutions

Flower drop (blossoms fall off without forming fruit)
The most common pepper frustration. Caused by nighttime temps below 55°F or daytime temps above 95°F, low humidity, inconsistent watering, or too much nitrogen fertilizer. Provide consistent conditions and be patient — peppers often drop their first flush of flowers and set fruit heavily on the second round as temperatures stabilize.
Aphids
Small soft-bodied insects that cluster on new growth and leaf undersides. They secrete sticky honeydew that attracts ants and encourages sooty mold. Blast with water, spray neem oil weekly, or introduce ladybugs. Inspect plants regularly — catching aphids early makes them easy to control.
Sunscald (white or tan papery patches on fruit)
Caused by direct, intense sun on exposed fruit. This often happens after pruning removes too much leaf canopy. Healthy leaf coverage shades developing fruit. If sunscald is a problem, avoid heavy pruning and use shade cloth during the hottest part of the day.
Blossom end rot
Same cause as in tomatoes — inconsistent watering prevents calcium uptake. Water deeply and consistently. Mulch to retain soil moisture. Avoid over-fertilizing with nitrogen, which promotes leaves at the expense of fruit. More common in bell peppers than in hot varieties.
Slow or stunted growth
Almost always caused by cold soil or low temperatures. Peppers simply stop growing below 60°F. If spring is cold, keep seedlings indoors longer. Black plastic mulch over the soil warms it 5–10°F and can make a significant difference in early-season growth. Avoid planting until the soil has truly warmed.

Best Spaces for Peppers

Peppers are excellent container crops and adapt well to various growing environments:

Balcony

3–5 gallon containers in full sun. Heat reflected from walls and floors benefits peppers.

Backyard

Raised beds or rows with 18-inch spacing. Black plastic mulch warms the soil for faster growth.

Indoor Setup

Grow lights make year-round pepper production possible. Compact varieties like Thai and jalapeño do best.
