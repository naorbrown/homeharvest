Tomatoes

Cherry, patio, and heirloom varieties. The most rewarding container crop — nothing at the store compares to a vine-ripened homegrown tomato.

Overview & Varieties

Tomatoes are the crown jewel of home food growing. A single cherry tomato plant in a 5-gallon container can produce 200+ fruits over the course of a summer. The flavor difference between a store-bought tomato (picked green, shipped 1,500 miles, gas-ripened) and one you pick warm from the vine is not subtle — it is a completely different experience.

Understanding the two growth types is essential:

  • Determinate (bush) — These grow to a fixed height (3–4 feet), produce all their fruit in a concentrated 2–3 week window, then stop. Best for containers, small spaces, and canning. Varieties: “Patio,” “Bush Early Girl,” “Roma.”
  • Indeterminate (vine) — These keep growing and producing fruit all season until frost kills them. They need staking or caging and more space, but produce far more total fruit. Varieties: “Sungold,” “Sweet 100,” “Brandywine,” “Cherokee Purple.”

The best varieties for home growing:

  • Cherry and grape tomatoes — The most productive and forgiving type for beginners. “Sungold” produces intensely sweet, orange fruits. “Sweet 100” lives up to its name with massive clusters. Both are indeterminate and prolific.
  • Patio tomatoes — Bred specifically for containers. “Patio Princess” and “Tumbling Tom” stay compact (2–3 feet) and produce without staking. Determinate, so they’re hands-off once established.
  • Slicers — Full-sized tomatoes for sandwiches and salads. “Better Boy” and “Early Girl” are reliable producers. Need 5-gallon minimum containers and strong stakes.
  • Heirlooms — “Brandywine,” “Cherokee Purple,” “Black Krim” — incomparable flavor but lower yields and more disease susceptibility. Worth growing for the taste alone, but not the best choice for beginners.

Growing Requirements

Light 8+ hours direct sun (non-negotiable)
Water Deep watering every 2–3 days
Soil Rich potting mix with compost
Temperature 65–85°F (18–29°C)

Tomatoes are sun-hungry plants. Less than 6 hours of direct sun means drastically reduced fruit production and increased disease pressure. If you have a shady space, choose a different crop. For containers, use at least a 5-gallon pot — tomatoes have extensive root systems, and undersized containers lead to constant watering stress.

Step-by-Step Growing Guide

  1. Start seeds indoors 6–8 weeks before last frost. Plant seeds ¼ inch deep in seed-starting mix. Keep soil temperature at 70–80°F for germination (a heat mat helps enormously). Seeds germinate in 5–10 days. Provide strong light immediately — a south-facing window or grow light 2 inches above seedlings for 14–16 hours per day.
  2. Pot up seedlings. When seedlings have their first set of true leaves (the second pair, not the initial seed leaves), transplant into 4-inch pots. Bury the stem up to the first leaves — tomatoes root along buried stems, building a stronger plant. This is unique to tomatoes and peppers.
  3. Harden off before transplanting outdoors. One week before planting outside, gradually expose seedlings to outdoor conditions. Start with 1–2 hours in a sheltered, shady spot and increase daily. This prevents transplant shock that can set plants back by weeks.
  4. Transplant when nighttime temps stay above 50°F. Dig a hole deep enough to bury two-thirds of the stem. Add a handful of compost and a pinch of bone meal to the hole. Water deeply immediately after planting. Space plants 24–36 inches apart, or one plant per 5-gallon container.
  5. Stake or cage immediately. Install support at planting time, not later. Cages work for determinate varieties. Indeterminate types need sturdy stakes (6+ feet) or tall cages. Tying plants to their support every 8–12 inches as they grow prevents branches from snapping under the weight of fruit.
  6. Prune suckers on indeterminate varieties. Suckers are shoots that grow in the “armpit” between the main stem and a branch. Pinch them off when small (under 2 inches) to direct energy into fruit production rather than foliage. Determinate tomatoes should not be pruned — their fruit sets on all branches.
  7. Water deeply at the base. Water at soil level, never on the leaves. Overhead watering promotes fungal disease. Water deeply every 2–3 days rather than lightly every day — this encourages deep root growth. Consistent watering is critical once fruit begins to set.

Pruning & Training Guide

Proper pruning makes the difference between a scraggly plant with a handful of tomatoes and a productive powerhouse. These techniques apply primarily to indeterminate varieties:

  • Remove suckers below the first flower cluster. This directs energy upward to the fruiting branches and improves airflow at the base of the plant, reducing disease pressure.
  • Limit main stems. Allow 1–3 main stems on indeterminate plants. More stems means more but smaller fruit. Fewer stems means fewer but larger fruit. For cherry tomatoes, 2–3 stems is ideal. For slicers and heirlooms, 1–2 stems produces the largest fruit.
  • Remove lower leaves as they yellow. As the plant grows, lower leaves that touch the soil or get no light should be removed. This improves airflow and removes the entry point for soil-borne diseases that splash onto lower foliage during rain.
  • Top the plant 4 weeks before first frost. Clip the growing tip of each stem to stop new growth and redirect energy into ripening existing fruit. Any flowers that set within 4 weeks of frost won’t have time to produce ripe fruit.

Common Problems & Solutions

Blossom end rot (black, sunken spot on bottom of fruit)
Caused by calcium deficiency, which is almost always triggered by inconsistent watering — not a lack of calcium in the soil. The plant can’t transport calcium to the fruit when water supply fluctuates. Water deeply and consistently. Mulch to retain moisture. Avoid heavy nitrogen fertilizers, which exacerbate the issue.
Tomato hornworms
Large green caterpillars (3–4 inches) that can strip a plant in days. Hand-pick and relocate them. Look for dark droppings on leaves below — follow them upward to find the worm. If you see white cocoons on a hornworm, leave it — those are parasitic wasp eggs that will kill it and produce more wasps to protect your garden.
Early blight (brown spots with concentric rings on lower leaves)
A common fungal disease that starts on lower leaves and works upward. Remove affected leaves immediately, improve airflow by pruning, and mulch to prevent soil splash. Water at the base, never overhead. Avoid working with plants when foliage is wet. Copper-based fungicide sprays can slow the spread.
Fruit cracking
Happens when heavy rain or deep watering follows a dry spell. The fruit expands faster than the skin can stretch. Keep watering consistent — this is the single most important tomato growing habit. Mulch 3–4 inches deep to buffer soil moisture. Pick fruit that starts to crack immediately; it won’t heal and will attract insects.
Flower drop (blossoms fall off without setting fruit)
Usually caused by temperatures above 90°F or below 55°F at night. Tomato pollen becomes non-viable in extreme heat. There’s little you can do except wait for temperatures to moderate. Shade cloth can help in extreme heat. The plant will resume setting fruit when conditions improve.

Best Spaces for Tomatoes

Tomatoes need maximum sun but can thrive in surprisingly small footprints:

Balcony

5-gallon containers on a sun-drenched, south-facing balcony. Cherry and patio types excel here.

Backyard

Raised beds or in-ground rows with full sun. Grow the big heirlooms you can’t fit on a balcony.
