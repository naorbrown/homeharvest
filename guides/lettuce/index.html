Lettuce & Greens

Loose-leaf lettuce, arugula, spinach, kale, and swiss chard. Cut-and-come-again harvesting keeps producing for weeks.

Overview & Varieties

Lettuce and salad greens are among the most productive crops you can grow at home. A single 12-inch container of leaf lettuce produces enough salad for one person every week, and because you harvest outer leaves while the plant keeps growing from the center, one planting produces for 6–8 weeks before it needs replacing.

The best varieties for home growing:

  • Leaf lettuce — The easiest and most productive type. Varieties like “Salad Bowl,” “Red Sails,” and “Oak Leaf” grow quickly and tolerate cut-and-come-again harvesting better than heading types. Ready in 30–40 days.
  • Romaine — Upright, crunchy heads with excellent heat tolerance compared to other lettuces. Takes 60–70 days for full heads, but you can harvest outer leaves much sooner. “Little Gem” is a compact variety ideal for containers.
  • Butterhead — Soft, buttery leaves that form loose heads. “Bibb” and “Buttercrunch” are popular. Slightly more cold-tolerant than leaf types. 50–60 days to full head.
  • Arugula — Peppery, fast-growing, and extremely productive. Direct sow and harvest baby leaves in just 21 days. Bolts quickly in heat, so plant in spring or fall. Succession plant every 2 weeks.
  • Spinach — Nutrient-dense and cold-tolerant. Prefers cool weather and bolts rapidly in temperatures above 75°F. “Bloomsdale Long Standing” is the most bolt-resistant variety. Ready in 35–45 days.
  • Kale — The hardiest green in this group. Survives light frosts and actually tastes sweeter after cold exposure. “Lacinato” (dinosaur kale) and “Red Russian” are excellent container varieties. Harvest outer leaves continuously for months.
  • Swiss chard — Colorful stems in red, yellow, and white. More heat-tolerant than lettuce or spinach, making it a good summer green. Harvest outer stalks at the base. Produces all season long.

Growing Requirements

Light 4–6 hours sun (partial shade OK)
Water Consistent moisture, never waterlogged
Soil Rich, well-draining potting mix
Temperature 45–75°F (7–24°C)

Greens are one of the few crops that actually prefer partial shade, especially in warmer climates. Four hours of morning sun with afternoon shade produces excellent lettuce. In full sun, plants bolt faster and leaves become bitter. Keep soil consistently moist — lettuce is 95% water and wilts quickly when dry.

Step-by-Step Planting Guide

  1. Start from seed — direct sow. Greens grow best when direct-sown rather than transplanted. Scatter seeds on the surface of moistened potting mix or garden soil. Press lightly and cover with just ¼ inch of fine soil. Lettuce seeds need light to germinate, so don’t bury them deep.
  2. Thin seedlings to proper spacing. Once seedlings have 2–3 true leaves, thin to 4–6 inches apart for leaf lettuce, 8–10 inches for romaine and kale. Don’t waste the thinnings — eat them as baby greens. Crowded plants produce smaller leaves and are more prone to disease.
  3. Water consistently. Greens need steady, even moisture. Water at the base of plants rather than overhead to reduce disease risk. Mulch around plants with straw or shredded leaves to retain moisture and keep roots cool.
  4. Feed lightly. Apply a balanced liquid fertilizer at half strength every 2–3 weeks. Too much nitrogen makes lettuce grow fast but can increase bitterness. Compost worked into the soil at planting time often provides sufficient nutrition for the entire growing season.
  5. Harvest outer leaves first. Begin harvesting when leaves are 4–6 inches long. Always pick from the outside of the plant, leaving the center crown and inner leaves to continue growing. This cut-and-come-again method extends your harvest by weeks. Harvest in the morning when leaves are most crisp and hydrated.

Preventing Bolting

Bolting — when plants send up a flower stalk — is the number one frustration with greens. Once a plant bolts, leaves turn bitter and production stops. Here’s how to delay it:

  • Shade cloth: A 30–50% shade cloth over your greens in summer reduces temperatures by 10–15°F and can add weeks to your harvest window. Even a strategically placed patio umbrella helps.
  • Succession planting: Instead of planting one large batch, sow a small amount every 2–3 weeks from early spring through fall. By the time one planting bolts, the next is ready to harvest. This is the single most effective strategy for continuous greens.
  • Heat-tolerant varieties: Choose bolt-resistant cultivars like “Jericho” romaine, “Muir” lettuce, or “New Zealand” spinach (a heat-loving spinach substitute). These buy you an extra 2–3 weeks in warm weather.
  • Mulch and morning harvest: Mulch keeps soil cool, and harvesting in the morning reduces heat stress on plants. Both small steps that compound over the season.

Common Problems & Solutions

Bolting in heat
The most common issue. When temperatures consistently exceed 75–80°F, lettuce and spinach will flower. Use shade cloth, choose heat-tolerant varieties, and succession plant. Once a plant bolts, pull it out and replace it — the leaves are too bitter to enjoy.
Slugs and snails
These pests love greens and feed at night, leaving ragged holes in leaves and slimy trails. Set out beer traps (shallow dishes of beer at soil level), sprinkle diatomaceous earth around plants, or hand-pick at dusk. Copper tape around container rims also deters them.
Aphids on leaves
Small green or black insects clustering on leaf undersides. A strong blast of water from a hose knocks most off. For persistent infestations, spray with diluted neem oil or insecticidal soap. Encourage ladybugs and lacewings, which are voracious aphid predators.
Bitter-tasting leaves
Caused by heat stress, inconsistent watering, or over-mature leaves. Harvest younger leaves for the mildest flavor. Water consistently and provide afternoon shade in warm weather. If leaves taste bitter, soaking them in ice water for 10 minutes before eating helps reduce bitterness.
Tip burn (brown leaf edges)
Caused by calcium deficiency or irregular watering. Keep moisture levels consistent — don’t let soil dry out completely between waterings. In containers, ensure good drainage so roots get both water and air.

Best Spaces for Lettuce & Greens

Greens are versatile and grow well in nearly any space with partial sun:

Windowsill

A wide sill or window box grows enough salad greens for daily harvesting. East-facing is ideal.

Balcony

Railing planters and grow bags along the railing. Partial shade from upper floors is actually an advantage.

Backyard

Raised beds or in-ground rows. Shade cloth extends the season through summer heat.
