Overview & Varieties

One 12-inch container of leaf lettuce gives you a salad a week.

Greens are among the most productive home crops. Cut-and-come-again harvesting means one planting produces for 6–8 weeks.

Best varieties for home growing:

  • Leaf lettuce — Easiest and most productive. Salad Bowl, Red Sails, and Oak Leaf are ready in 30–40 days.
  • Romaine — Crunchy and heat-tolerant. Little Gem is great for containers.
  • Butterhead — Soft, buttery leaves. Bibb and Buttercrunch in 50–60 days.
  • Arugula — Peppery and fast. Harvest baby leaves in just 21 days.
  • Spinach — Nutrient-dense, cold-tolerant. Bloomsdale Long Standing resists bolting best.
  • Kale — Hardiest green here. Survives frost, tastes sweeter after cold.
  • Swiss chard — Colorful stems, heat-tolerant. Produces all season long.
Growing Requirements
Light 4–6 hours sun (partial shade OK)
Water Consistent moisture, never waterlogged
Soil Rich, well-draining potting mix
Temperature 45–75°F (7–24°C)

Greens actually prefer partial shade. 4 hours of morning sun with afternoon shade is ideal.

Full sun causes faster bolting and bitter leaves. Keep soil consistently moist — lettuce is 95% water.

Step-by-Step Planting Guide
  1. Direct sow seeds. Scatter on moistened soil, press lightly, cover with ¼ inch of fine soil. Lettuce needs light to germinate — don’t bury deep.
  2. Thin seedlings. At 2–3 true leaves, thin to 4–6 inches apart. Eat the thinnings as baby greens.
  3. Water at the base. Keep moisture steady and even. Mulch with straw to retain moisture and cool roots.
  4. Feed lightly. Half-strength liquid fertilizer every 2–3 weeks. Too much nitrogen increases bitterness.
  5. Harvest outer leaves first. Pick at 4–6 inches long, always from the outside. Leave the center crown growing for weeks of repeat harvests.
Preventing Bolting

Bolting means the plant sends up a flower stalk. Leaves turn bitter and production stops.

  • Shade cloth: 30–50% shade reduces temps by 10–15°F. Even a patio umbrella helps.
  • Succession planting: Sow small amounts every 2–3 weeks. When one batch bolts, the next is ready.
Succession plant every 2–3 weeks for continuous greens.
  • Heat-tolerant varieties: Jericho romaine and Muir lettuce buy 2–3 extra weeks in warm weather.
  • Mulch and morning harvest: Mulch cools soil; morning picking reduces heat stress.
Common Problems & Solutions
Bolting in heat
Above 75–80°F, lettuce and spinach flower. Use shade cloth, heat-tolerant varieties, and succession planting.
Slugs and snails
Night feeders that leave ragged holes. Beer traps, diatomaceous earth, or copper tape on container rims.
Aphids on leaves
Small insects on leaf undersides. Blast off with water or spray diluted neem oil.
Bitter-tasting leaves
From heat, dry soil, or old leaves. Harvest young, water consistently, and soak in ice water before eating.
Tip burn (brown edges)
Calcium deficiency or irregular watering. Keep moisture consistent and ensure good drainage.
Best Spaces for Lettuce & Greens

Greens grow in nearly any space with partial sun.

Windowsill

A wide sill or window box grows enough salad greens for daily harvesting. East-facing is ideal.

Balcony

Railing planters and grow bags along the railing. Partial shade from upper floors is actually an advantage.

Backyard

Raised beds or in-ground rows. Shade cloth extends the season through summer heat.
