Herbs

Basil, cilantro, mint, parsley, rosemary, and dill. The fastest, most rewarding entry point into home growing. Harvest in weeks, not months.

Overview & Varieties

Herbs are the single best starting point for any home grower. They require minimal space, grow quickly, and provide immediate culinary value that you can use every day. A single basil plant in a windowsill produces more than a grocery store bunch every week.

The best beginner herbs fall into two categories:

  • Annual herbs — Basil, cilantro, dill, and parsley. These grow fast from seed, produce heavily for one season, then bolt (go to flower and seed). Succession plant every 3–4 weeks for continuous supply.
  • Perennial herbs — Rosemary, thyme, mint, oregano, and chives. These come back year after year. Slower to establish but lower maintenance long-term. Mint spreads aggressively — always grow it in its own container.

Growing Requirements

Light 6–8 hours direct sun
Water When top inch is dry
Soil Well-draining potting mix
Temperature 60–80°F (15–27°C)

Most herbs prefer slightly dry conditions over wet. Overwatering is the most common beginner mistake. Use pots with drainage holes and let the soil dry between waterings.

Step-by-Step Planting Guide

  1. Choose your container. Any pot 6–8 inches deep with drainage holes works. Terra cotta breathes well for herbs. One herb per 6-inch pot, or combine compatible herbs (basil + parsley) in a 12-inch planter.
  2. Fill with potting mix. Use a quality indoor potting mix. Avoid garden soil — it compacts in containers and drains poorly. Fill to 1 inch below the rim.
  3. Plant seeds or starts. Seeds: scatter on surface, cover with ¼ inch of soil, and mist gently. Starts: dig a hole the size of the root ball, place the plant, and firm soil around it. Water thoroughly.
  4. Position for light. South-facing windowsill is ideal. East or west-facing works for most herbs. If natural light is limited, a basic LED grow light at 12–16 hours per day is sufficient.
  5. Water consistently. Check soil daily by pressing your finger 1 inch into the soil. If dry, water until it drains from the bottom. If moist, wait another day. Herbs prefer slightly dry to soggy.
  6. Feed monthly. Use a diluted all-purpose liquid fertilizer (half strength) once per month during the growing season. Over-fertilizing herbs actually reduces flavor intensity.
  7. Harvest regularly. Begin harvesting when plants have at least 3 sets of true leaves. Regular harvesting encourages bushier growth. Never remove more than one-third of the plant at once.

Harvest Timing & Technique

The key to continuous herb production is harvesting correctly:

  • Basil: Pinch stems just above a leaf node (where two leaves emerge). This forces two new stems to grow from that node, doubling your production. Remove flower buds immediately — once basil flowers, leaf flavor diminishes.
  • Cilantro: Cut outer stems at the base. Cilantro bolts quickly in heat — succession plant every 2–3 weeks. Once it bolts, let it go to seed (coriander) and collect the seeds for replanting.
  • Mint: Cut stems freely — mint is nearly impossible to over-harvest. Pinch growing tips to encourage bushy growth rather than leggy runners.
  • Parsley: Cut outer stalks at the base, leaving the center crown to continue producing. Flat-leaf (Italian) has stronger flavor than curly varieties.
  • Rosemary: Snip stem tips (3–4 inches). Avoid cutting into old, woody growth — it won’t regrow from bare wood.

Common Problems & Solutions

Leggy, stretched growth
Not enough light. Move to a brighter location or add a grow light. Stems reaching toward the window is a clear sign. Herbs need at minimum 6 hours of direct sun.
Yellow leaves at the base
Overwatering. Let the soil dry more between waterings. Check that your pot has drainage holes and that the saucer isn’t holding standing water.
Wilting despite moist soil
Root rot from overwatering. If roots are brown and mushy, the plant may not recover. Repot in fresh, dry soil and reduce watering frequency significantly.
Sudden flowering (bolting)
Triggered by heat, stress, or long days. Cilantro and basil bolt first. Remove flower buds immediately. For cilantro, succession plant to always have young plants ready.
Small white flies or sticky leaves
Whiteflies or aphids. Spray with diluted neem oil solution or wipe leaves with soapy water. Isolate affected plants to prevent spread. Increase airflow around plants.

Best Spaces for Herbs

Herbs thrive in almost any space. Here are the best setups:

Windowsill

The ideal herb setup. South-facing window, 2–3 pots, daily harvesting.

Balcony

Railing planters or grouped containers. More sun exposure than indoors.

Indoor Setup

Grow lights let you grow herbs anywhere year-round, regardless of windows.
