Microgreens

Sunflower, radish, broccoli, and pea shoots. The fastest harvest in gardening — 7 to 14 days from seed to plate.

Overview & Varieties

Microgreens are seedlings of vegetables and herbs harvested just after the first true leaves appear — typically 7 to 14 days after planting. They pack concentrated flavor and nutrition into a tiny package, often containing 4 to 40 times the nutrients of their mature counterparts. You can grow them on any countertop, in any season, with no outdoor space required.

The best varieties for beginners:

  • Sunflower — Nutty, crunchy, and substantial. Among the largest microgreens, making them easy to handle and harvest. Soak seeds 8–12 hours before planting for best germination. Ready in 10–12 days.
  • Radish — Spicy and fast. The fastest-growing microgreen — ready in 5–7 days. Bright pink stems add color to any dish. No pre-soaking needed.
  • Broccoli — Mild, slightly bitter flavor. One of the most nutrient-dense microgreens available, rich in sulforaphane. Ready in 8–10 days. Seeds are tiny, so spread them densely.
  • Pea shoots — Sweet and tender with a fresh pea flavor. Produces the tallest microgreens, often 3–4 inches. Soak seeds overnight before planting. Ready in 10–14 days. Can be harvested twice — a second crop regrows from the cut stems.
  • Wheatgrass — Juiced rather than eaten whole. Grows tall and dense. Ready in 10–12 days. Primarily used for juicing, not salads.

Growing Requirements

Light Indirect light or windowsill
Water Mist 2x daily with spray bottle
Growing Medium 1 inch potting soil, coconut coir, or hemp mat
Temperature 65–75°F (18–24°C)

Microgreens do not need direct sunlight — bright indirect light or a windowsill works perfectly. Strong direct sun can actually wilt delicate seedlings. A standard 10×20 inch nursery tray is the most common container, but any shallow dish with drainage works.

Step-by-Step Growing Guide

  1. Soak seeds overnight (if needed). Large seeds like sunflower, pea, and wheatgrass germinate much faster after 8–12 hours of soaking. Smaller seeds like broccoli and radish can be planted dry.
  2. Prepare your tray. Fill a shallow tray with about 1 inch of moistened growing medium. Potting soil, coconut coir, or hemp mats all work. The medium should be damp but not waterlogged — squeeze a handful and only a few drops should come out.
  3. Spread seeds densely. Microgreens are planted much more densely than garden plants. Scatter seeds in a single layer across the entire surface, leaving almost no gaps. For reference, a 10×20 tray uses about 1–2 ounces of seed depending on variety.
  4. Cover for 2–3 days (blackout phase). Place another tray or a sheet of cardboard over the seeds. This darkness forces the seedlings to push upward, developing strong stems. Mist once daily during this phase. Some growers place a light weight on top to encourage sturdy growth.
  5. Uncover and expose to light. After 2–3 days, seedlings should be ½ to 1 inch tall and pale yellow. Remove the cover and place the tray near a window or under a grow light. The seedlings will green up within 24 hours as they begin photosynthesizing.
  6. Mist daily until harvest. Mist the surface 1–2 times per day, keeping the growing medium moist but never soaked. Bottom watering (pouring water into a solid tray beneath the growing tray) is even better for preventing mold.
  7. Harvest at 1–2 inches tall. When the first true leaves appear (the second set of leaves, after the initial seed leaves), your microgreens are ready. Cut them just above the soil line with clean scissors. Harvest the entire tray at once or in sections over 2–3 days. Rinse and eat immediately for maximum freshness.

Common Problems & Solutions

Mold or fuzzy white growth
The most common microgreen issue, caused by overwatering, poor airflow, or seeds planted too densely. Reduce misting frequency, increase air circulation with a small fan, and switch to bottom watering. If mold appears, it usually means the tray is too wet. A light dusting of mold on roots is normal and harmless — true mold has a musty smell and spreads across the surface.
Leggy, thin growth
Not enough light. Move the tray closer to the window or add a basic grow light. Microgreens stretch toward available light, becoming tall and weak-stemmed. They need at least 4–6 hours of indirect light after the blackout phase.
Uneven germination
Usually caused by uneven seed distribution or inconsistent moisture. Make sure seeds are spread in a single, even layer and that the growing medium is uniformly damp. Some seed varieties naturally germinate at different rates — radish is fast (2 days), sunflower is slower (3–4 days).
Slow growth in cold temperatures
Microgreens need warmth to germinate and grow quickly. Below 60°F, growth slows significantly. Place trays in the warmest room of your home, away from cold drafts. A seedling heat mat can speed germination by 2–3 days in cold environments.
Seeds not germinating
Old or improperly stored seeds lose viability. Always buy seeds labeled for sprouting or microgreen use. Store unused seeds in a cool, dry place. If seeds are more than 2 years old, test germination by sprouting a small batch on a wet paper towel before planting a full tray.

Best Spaces for Microgreens

Microgreens grow in the smallest spaces imaginable. Here are the best setups:

Windowsill

A single tray on a windowsill produces a continuous supply. Rotate trays weekly for steady harvests.

Indoor Setup

A shelving unit with grow lights lets you stack multiple trays vertically. Scale to any production level.
