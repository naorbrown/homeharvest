Overview

Soil beds are the simplest and most forgiving growing system. Billions of microorganisms naturally cycle nutrients, retain moisture, and support root health.

Two types. The right choice depends on your yard and your body:

  • In-ground beds — Remove turf, amend with compost, plant directly. Cheapest option with unlimited root depth.
  • Raised beds — Build a frame and fill with controlled soil mix. Best when native soil is clay, contaminated, or poorly drained.

Choose in-ground for decent soil and low cost. Choose raised for guaranteed quality and better drainage.

Cost Breakdown

The most affordable growing system. Here is what to expect:

In-ground bed
Nearly free with decent soil. Compost + seeds = $45 or less.
Raised bed (4×8 ft)
Lumber, hardware, and soil fill totals $100–150. Lasts 5–10 years.

Costs drop for the second bed since you already have tools. Start with one and expand each season.

Step-by-Step Setup Guide

Soil is a living ecosystem of microorganisms that no other system can replicate.

  1. Test your soil. A $10–15 home test kit checks pH (target 6.0–7.0) and nutrient levels. Skipping this leads to disappointing first harvests.
  2. Choose the sunniest location. Vegetables need at least 6 hours of direct sun; 8 is better. Avoid spots near large trees.
  3. Mark your bed dimensions. A 4×8 ft bed is ideal. You can reach the center from either side without stepping on the soil.
  4. Assemble the frame (raised beds). Join untreated lumber with deck screws or corner brackets. Use cedar for the longest life.
  5. Level the ground. Takes 15 minutes and prevents pooling water at one end.
  6. Line with hardware cloth. Staple ¼-inch cloth to the bottom to block burrowing pests.
  7. Fill with soil mix. Use 60% topsoil, 30% compost, 10% perlite. Fill to within 1–2 inches of the top.
  8. In-ground: remove sod and double-dig. Loosen 24 inches of soil and mix the top layer with 3–4 inches of compost.
Best Crops

Soil beds grow the full range of home food crops. Best starting points:

Herbs

Basil, cilantro, mint, parsley. Harvest in weeks. Dedicate one corner of the bed.

Lettuce & Greens

Cut-and-come-again varieties thrive in beds with consistent moisture.

Tomatoes

Deep roots spread freely. Beds produce the largest tomato harvests.

Peppers

Love the warmth of raised beds. Great companions for tomatoes.

Microgreens

Direct-sow densely in a small section for continuous harvests.

Root Vegetables

Carrots, radishes, beets. The deep loose soil of beds is ideal.
Maintenance Schedule
Weekly
Water deeply (1 inch per week). Pull weeds while small and check for pest damage.
Monthly
Side-dress plants with a 1-inch ring of compost or organic fertilizer.
Seasonally
Add 2–3 inches of mulch around plants. Rotate crop families at season end.
Annually
Top-dress with 1–2 inches of compost in early spring. Re-test soil every 1–2 years.
Common Problems & Solutions

Never walk on your growing beds — foot traffic crushes the air pockets roots need.

Compacted soil
Never step on beds. Lay a board across to distribute weight if you must reach the center.
Poor drainage
Water pooling for hours means too much clay. Even 6 inches of elevation via raised beds fixes this.
Nutrient depletion
Pale, stunted plants signal spent soil. Rotate crop families annually and amend with compost each season.
Weeds
3 inches of mulch blocks most weed seeds. Pull survivors while small — never let them go to seed.
Grubs and soil pests
Plants wilting despite water? Check for C-shaped white larvae at the roots. Apply beneficial nematodes as biological control.
