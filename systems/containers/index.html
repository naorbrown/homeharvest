Overview

Container gardening is growing food in any vessel that holds soil and drains water. Pots, grow bags, buckets, window boxes — if it drains, it counts.

You do not need a yard. A single pot on a windowsill is enough.

Containers work on fire escapes, rooftops, stoops, driveways, and indoors near a window. Portability is the superpower — chase the sun, dodge frost, rearrange anytime.

The tradeoff is capacity: less soil means more frequent watering and feeding. But the barrier to entry is near zero, and one pot often becomes a full balcony within a season.

Container Types
Terra cotta pots
Porous clay that breathes, keeping roots oxygenated and reducing rot risk. Dries out fast, heavy, and cracks in freezing temps — best for herbs like rosemary and thyme.
Plastic pots
Lightweight, cheap ($2–5), and retain moisture longer than clay. Look for multiple drainage holes in the bottom.
Fabric grow bags
Breathable fabric that air-prunes roots, creating denser root systems. A 5-pack of 5-gallon bags costs $8–15 — the best value in container gardening.
5-gallon buckets
Free from bakeries (ask for old frosting buckets) or $3–5 at hardware stores. Drill 5–6 holes in the bottom and grow full-sized tomatoes, peppers, or eggplant.
Window boxes
Long planters for railings and windowsills — ideal for herbs, lettuce, and strawberries. Limited to 6–8 inches of soil depth, so stick to shallow-rooted crops.

Sizing Guide
Herbs 6–8 inch pot
Lettuce / greens 8–10 inch pot
Tomatoes 5-gallon minimum
Peppers 3–5 gallon
Root vegetables 12+ inches deep

When in doubt, go bigger. More soil means more moisture, less watering, and healthier plants.

Cost Breakdown
Repurposed containers
Old buckets, yogurt tubs, or storage bins with holes drilled in the bottom. Cost: nearly free.
Basic plastic pots
Standard nursery pots from garden centers: $2–5 each. Three or four gets you started for under $20.
Fabric grow bags
5-pack of 5-gallon grow bags: $8–15. Five full-sized containers for the price of one terra cotta pot.
Quality potting mix
A bag (1–2 cu ft): $10–15. Enough for several small pots or one large container.

Total startup cost: $10–30 for containers and soil to grow your first food.

Step-by-Step Setup
  1. Ensure drainage holes. No holes = dead plants. For buckets, drill 5–6 quarter-inch holes in the bottom.
  2. Match container to crop. 6–8 inch pots for herbs, 5-gallon for tomatoes and peppers. When unsure, size up.
  3. Fill with potting mix. Use commercial potting mix, never garden soil. Fill to 1 inch below the rim.
  4. Plant seeds or transplants. Follow packet depth for seeds. For transplants, match the original soil line and water immediately.
  5. Water until it drains. Water slowly until it flows from the bottom holes. This settles soil around roots.
  6. Maximize light. Aim for 6+ hours of direct sun daily. South-facing spots are ideal; LED grow lights work indoors.
  7. Add saucers. Catch runoff and protect surfaces. Empty them within 30 minutes so roots do not sit in water.
Drainage & Watering

Improper drainage is the #1 killer in container gardening.

No drainage holes = dead plants. Waterlogged roots rot and no amount of watering saves them.

  • Drill holes if there are none. Every container needs at least one drainage hole in the bottom.
  • Check moisture daily. Finger 1 inch into soil — dry means water, moist means wait.
  • Containers dry fast. A 6-inch pot in summer sun can dry out in a single day. If you water daily and plants still wilt, size up.
  • Self-watering containers help. Built-in reservoirs wick water to roots. Great for busy schedules or vacations.
  • Try bottom-watering. Set the pot in a basin of water for 20–30 minutes. Prevents compaction and ensures even moisture.
Best Crops

Match container size to the plant and nearly anything grows in a container.

Herbs

The perfect container crop. A 6-inch pot produces enough basil for weekly cooking.

Lettuce & Greens

Shallow roots thrive in 8–10 inch containers. Cut-and-come-again for weeks.

Tomatoes

Patio and cherry varieties in 5-gallon containers produce heavily all season.

Peppers

Compact pepper varieties are ideal for 3–5 gallon containers on a sunny patio.

Microgreens

Grow in any shallow tray. Ready in 7–14 days. The ultimate container crop.
Common Problems & Solutions
Root-bound plants
Roots circling the pot and growing out of drainage holes. Fix: move to a container one size larger and loosen the root ball before repotting.
Salt buildup from fertilizer
White crust on soil or pot walls means mineral salt buildup. Flush each container with plain water once a month until it runs freely from the bottom.
Containers overheating in direct sun
Dark pots absorb heat and can cook roots above 95°F. Use light-colored pots, double-pot with an air gap, or switch to fabric grow bags.
Fungus gnats
Tiny black flies near the soil surface that breed in consistently moist soil. Let the top inch dry fully between waterings — larvae cannot survive dry conditions.
