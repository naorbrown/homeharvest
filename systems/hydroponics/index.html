Overview

Hydroponics grows plants without soil — nutrients dissolve directly in water and feed the roots. The simplest setup is a mason jar with a net pot, built in 10 minutes for under $5.

Roots don’t waste energy searching through soil. They get everything instantly from the nutrient solution.

The result: 30–50% faster growth and the highest yields per square foot of any method. A 60-day soil crop matures in 40 days hydroponically.

The tradeoff is attention. Water has no buffer — if pH drifts or nutrients run out, plants feel it within hours.

Types of Systems
Kratky Method (passive, no pump)
Simplest entry point. No pump, no electricity — fill a jar with nutrient solution once and harvest later.
Deep Water Culture (DWC)
Most popular home system. Like Kratky plus an air pump for oxygen — handles peppers, tomatoes, and faster growth.
Nutrient Film Technique (NFT)
Best for scaling up. Nutrient solution flows through channels past roots — fits 10+ plants in a small footprint but not recommended as a first system.

Where to start: Begin with Kratky — one head of lettuce in a mason jar. Then build a DWC bucket for more capacity.

Cost Breakdown
Basic Kratky setup
~$30 for 3–6 plants. Jars ($5), net pots ($5), nutrients ($12–15), clay pebbles ($8). Reuse everything indefinitely.
DWC single bucket
$50–80 total. Bucket ($5–8), air pump ($15–20), airstone ($5), media ($10), nutrients ($12–15), pH kit ($10–15). Grows one large plant or several herbs.
Multi-plant system
$150–400 depending on scale. Add $50–100 for a grow light if indoors. An EC/PPM meter ($15–25) becomes essential at this level.
Step-by-Step DWC Setup
  1. Get a 5-gallon bucket with lid. Food-grade, dark-colored. Light buckets grow algae — wrap in dark tape if needed.
  2. Drill holes for net pots. Use a 3-inch hole saw. One hole for a large plant, 3–4 holes for herbs and lettuce.
  3. Install the air pump and airstone. Airstone goes in the bucket bottom. Pump stays outside, above water level or with a check valve.
  4. Mix nutrient solution. Greens: EC 1.0–1.6. Fruiting plants: EC 1.6–2.4. Start low — you can add more but can’t remove.
  5. Adjust pH to 5.8 (range 5.5–6.5). Add pH Down a few drops at a time. Stir and retest after each addition.
  6. Add seedlings in net pots with clay pebbles. Net pot bottom should just touch the water level. Airstone bubbles will splash moisture to the roots.
  7. Top off water daily. Large plants drink over a gallon per day at peak. Top off with pH-adjusted water at half-strength nutrients.
pH & Nutrient Management

Master pH and nutrients, and your plants will outperform any soil garden.

Above pH 6.5, nutrient lockout begins — micronutrients become unavailable even though they’re in the water.

  • Check pH every 2–3 days. Plants push pH up over time — keep pH Down on hand.
  • Target range: 5.5–6.5. Aim for 5.8 and let it drift to 6.2 before adjusting back down.
  • EC/PPM monitoring. Rising EC means dilute with water. Falling EC means add nutrients. A meter costs $15–25.
  • Full reservoir change every 1–2 weeks. Dump, rinse, mix fresh. Water your houseplants with the old solution.
Best Crops

Hydroponics excels at fast-growing, compact crops. Start with these proven winners.

Herbs

Basil grows explosively in DWC. Mint, cilantro, and parsley thrive in Kratky jars.

Lettuce & Greens

The quintessential hydroponic crop. Harvest in 30 days from seed.

Peppers

Excellent in DWC. Compact varieties produce heavily indoors year-round.

Tomatoes are possible but advanced. Attempt only after you’ve successfully grown herbs and lettuce.

Maintenance Schedule
Daily
Check water level. Large plants drink 1+ gallon per day. Top off with pH-adjusted water.
Every 2–3 days
Test pH and adjust to 5.5–6.5. Check root color — white is healthy, brown/slimy means trouble.
Weekly
Inspect roots and airstone. Lift net pots — look for slime or foul odor. Confirm airstone is bubbling steadily.
Every 1–2 weeks
Full reservoir change. Dump, wipe bucket, rinse airstone, mix fresh nutrients. Prevents salt buildup.
Common Problems & Solutions

Most hydroponic problems trace back to one thing: pH outside 5.5–6.5.

pH drift
Most common issue. Check every 2–3 days and adjust with pH Down — wild swings mean your reservoir is too small.
Root rot
Brown, slimy roots with foul smell. Fix: replace airstone, add hydrogen peroxide (3 ml/gal), and keep water below 72°F.
Algae growth
Green slime where light hits nutrient water. Block all light with opaque buckets and tape — not fatal but competes for nutrients.
Nutrient lockout
Deficiency symptoms despite nutrients in the water. Almost always a pH problem — fix pH first, then do a full reservoir change if needed.
Pump failure
Roots lose oxygen within hours. Keep a spare pump — or lower water to expose roots as a temporary Kratky conversion.
