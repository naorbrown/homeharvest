Frequently Asked Questions

Straight answers to the questions every beginner asks. If you're overthinking it, start here.

When should I start my garden?

It depends on what you're growing and where you live. Cool-season crops (lettuce, spinach, radishes) can go out 4–6 weeks before your last frost date. Warm-season crops (tomatoes, peppers, basil) go out after the last frost.

If you're starting indoors, you can begin anytime. Microgreens and herbs on a windowsill don't care about the season.

The best time to start is now. Pick one easy crop and plant it today.
How much space do I need?

Less than you think. A windowsill (1–4 sq ft) is enough for herbs and microgreens. A balcony (4–40 sq ft) can produce tomatoes, peppers, and salad greens.

Even a single 5-gallon bucket on a sunny step can grow a full tomato plant through the season.

You don't need a yard. You need one container and a spot that gets sunlight.
What's the easiest thing to grow?

Herbs (basil, mint, chives) and microgreens are the most forgiving. Herbs grow in any container with decent light. Microgreens are ready in 7–14 days with minimal care.

For outdoor beginners, radishes and loose-leaf lettuce are almost impossible to kill. Radishes are harvestable in 25 days.

Start with herbs. They're cheap, fast, useful in the kitchen, and visibly droop when thirsty — so you learn watering instincts.
How often should I water?

There's no universal schedule. Check daily: push your finger 1 inch into the soil. If it's dry, water until it drains from the bottom. If it's moist, leave it alone.

Containers dry out faster than garden beds, especially in wind and heat. Small pots may need daily watering in summer. Raised beds typically need water every 2–3 days.

Overwatering kills more plants than underwatering. When in doubt, wait a day.
Do I need full sun?

Fruiting crops (tomatoes, peppers) need 6–8 hours of direct sun. Leafy greens and herbs can manage with 3–4 hours. Microgreens and sprouts need very little.

No sun at all? An indoor setup with a basic grow light ($20–40) removes the sunlight constraint entirely.

More light = more fruit. Less light = stick to leafy greens and herbs. No light = add a grow light.
How much does it cost to start?

A windowsill herb garden costs $10–30: a few pots, potting mix, and seeds. A balcony container setup runs $30–80. A raised bed garden starts at $50–150 depending on materials.

Seeds are the cheapest input. One $3 packet of basil seeds contains 200+ plants. Compare that to $3 for a single bundle of cut basil at the store.

Start small and cheap. A $10 herb setup teaches you everything you need before you scale up.
Can I grow food indoors?

Yes. Herbs, microgreens, lettuce, and sprouts all grow well indoors. A sunny windowsill is enough for herbs. An indoor setup with grow lights handles everything else.

Fruiting crops (tomatoes, peppers) are harder indoors — they need intense light and pollination. Stick to leafy greens and herbs indoors unless you're running grow lights.

Indoor growing is year-round growing. No frost, no pests, no weather. Start with microgreens — they're the fastest indoor win.
What basic tools do I need?

For containers: pots with drainage holes, saucers, potting mix, seeds, and a spray bottle. That's it.

For outdoor beds: add a hand trowel, a watering can or hose, and garden gloves. A soil test kit ($10–15) is useful but not required to start.

You don't need specialized tools. A container, soil, seeds, and water get you growing today.
How do I improve my soil?

For containers, use bagged indoor potting mix — never garden soil, which compacts and drains poorly in pots.

For outdoor beds, mix in compost. Two inches of compost worked into the top 6 inches of soil improves drainage, aeration, and nutrients. Do this once per season.

A simple soil pH test helps too. Most vegetables prefer pH 6.0–7.0. If your soil is too acidic, add garden lime. Too alkaline, add sulfur.

Good soil = healthy plants. Compost is the single best thing you can add to any garden bed.
What grows in shade?

Lettuce, spinach, kale, and other leafy greens tolerate partial shade (3–4 hours of sun). Mint and parsley also do fine with less light.

Microgreens need even less — indirect light works. Mushroom kits grow in dark corners.

Fruiting crops (tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers) won't produce well in shade. They need 6+ hours of direct sun.

Shade doesn't mean you can't grow. It means you grow leaves, not fruits.
How do I know when to harvest?

Each crop is different, but here are the basics:

  • Herbs — Pick once they have several sets of true leaves. Pinch from the top to encourage bushier growth.
  • Lettuce — Harvest outer leaves when they're 4–6 inches tall. The inner leaves keep growing.
  • Tomatoes — Wait until fully colored and slightly soft to touch. A ripe tomato releases easily from the vine.
  • Radishes — Pull when the root top pushes above the soil surface, usually 25–30 days.
When in doubt, harvest a little early. Most crops taste better young. Overgrown lettuce turns bitter, bolted herbs lose flavor.
How do I deal with pests without chemicals?

Prevention is easier than treatment. Healthy soil, proper spacing, and good airflow prevent most problems.

  • Aphids — Blast them off with a strong water spray. Repeat daily for a week.
  • Caterpillars — Hand-pick them. Check undersides of leaves in the morning.
  • Fungal issues — Water at the base, not on leaves. Improve air circulation. Remove affected leaves immediately.
  • Slugs — Copper tape around containers. Shallow dishes of beer as traps.

Companion planting helps too: basil repels aphids from tomatoes, marigolds deter many garden pests.

Most pest problems come from overcrowding and overwatering. Fix the environment before treating symptoms.
