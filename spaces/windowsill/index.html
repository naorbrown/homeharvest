Overview & What's Possible

A windowsill is the most accessible growing space. No landlord permission, no balcony, no yard required.

If your window gets a few hours of direct sun, you can grow food.

Two to four herb pots replace $5–15 per week in grocery store herbs.

A microgreen tray produces a dense handful of nutrition every 10–14 days. Lettuce containers give fresh salad handfuls weekly.

This won't replace a grocery run, but it builds the habit. Most serious growers started with one pot of basil.

Constraints are real: limited space, limited light, temperature swings near glass. But the setup is low-cost, low-risk, and immediately rewarding.

Best Crops

Stick to crops that tolerate limited root space and moderate light:

  • Herbs — Basil, cilantro, parsley, chives, and mint. One herb per 6-inch pot.
  • Microgreens — Best windowsill crop for nutrition density. Ready in 7–14 days in shallow trays.
  • Green onions — Regrow from store-bought roots in a glass of water. Harvest tops at 6–8 inches; they regrow 3–4 times.
  • Small lettuce varieties — Loose-leaf in a 6-inch pot with 4+ hours of sun. Harvest outer leaves for repeated cuts.

Avoid fruiting crops (tomatoes, peppers) on a windowsill. They need more light and root space than a sill provides.

Equipment Needed

Minimal gear required:

  • Small pots with drainage holes — 4–6 inch pots or a rectangular trough. Terra cotta or plastic both work.
  • Saucers or trays — Catch drainage to protect your sill. Empty standing water after 30 minutes.
  • Potting mix — Bagged indoor potting mix, not garden soil. Garden soil is too heavy and may contain pests.
  • Seeds or starter plants — Seeds are cheaper; starts are faster. One basil start plus one microgreen seed packet is a great combo.
  • Misting bottle — For watering seeds without disturbing the soil. Any cheap spray bottle works.

Total startup cost: $10–30.

Step-by-Step Setup Guide
  1. Assess your light. South-facing = best. East = gentle morning sun, good for herbs. North-facing rarely works without a grow light.
  2. Choose your containers. Measure your sill (most are 4–6 inches deep). Pick pots that fit without overhanging.
  3. Pick your first crops. Start with one or two plants, not six. Basil is ideal: fast-growing, useful, and droops visibly when thirsty.
  4. Set up your watering routine. Check soil daily. Finger 1 inch in — if dry, water until it drains from the bottom. Overwatering kills more windowsill plants than neglect.
Light Assessment

Light is the single biggest factor in windowsill success.

  • The shadow test. Hold your hand 12 inches above the sill at midday. Sharp shadow = strong direct light; no shadow = too diffused for food crops.
  • Light meter apps. Aim for 10,000 lux for herbs, 5,000 for microgreens. Below 5,000, add supplemental lighting.
  • Track hours, not just intensity. Most herbs need 4–6 hours of direct sun. Buildings and trees can block sun you assumed was there.
  • Seasonal shift. A window with 6 hours of sun in June may get 2 in December. Winter may require a clip-on LED grow light.
Maintenance Tips

A clean window transmits 10–15% more light than a dirty one.

  • Rotate pots weekly. Quarter turn, same direction each time. Prevents lopsided growth.
  • Clean windows regularly. Both sides of the glass, monthly during growing season.
  • Watch for drafts in winter. Warm-weather herbs suffer below 55°F. Move pots back from glass at night.
  • Harvest frequently. Regular cutting stimulates new growth. Unharvested herbs go leggy and bolt.
  • Feed lightly. Half-strength liquid fertilizer every 2–4 weeks. Skip in winter if growth has slowed.
Common Problems
Insufficient light
Signs: leggy stems, pale leaves, slow growth. Add a clip-on LED grow light ($15–25) positioned 6–12 inches above plants, running 10–14 hours daily.
Overwatering
If soil stays wet 2–3+ days, roots rot. Let the top inch dry before watering again. Yellow lower leaves and mushy stems mean too much water.
Temperature swings near glass
Summer sun through west-facing glass can cook plants. Winter nights near single-pane glass chill roots. Monitor temperature at plant level, not room level.
Root-bound plants in small pots
Roots circling the pot bottom means the plant outgrew its container. Move to a larger pot, or harvest aggressively and restart from seed.
