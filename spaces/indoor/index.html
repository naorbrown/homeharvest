Overview

LED grow lights eliminate window and season constraints entirely. A shelf with lights in any spare corner produces greens and herbs 365 days a year.

A small setup displaces $20–40 per month in grocery produce.

The tradeoff is upfront cost and electricity. You are replacing free sunlight with purchased light.

Indoor growing complements outdoor growing. The best indoor crops — greens, herbs, microgreens — are what you buy most often at the store.

Best Crops

Focus on short, fast-turnover crops with low light demands:

  • Herbs — Basil, cilantro, parsley, chives, and mint all thrive under grow lights. Basil is the star: fast-growing and immediately useful.
  • Microgreens — The ideal indoor crop. Shallow trays, 7–14 day turnaround, and high nutrition density.
  • Lettuce and greens — Loose-leaf lettuce, spinach, arugula, and kale stay compact under lights. Harvest outer leaves for ongoing production.

Avoid fruiting crops indoors — they need 2–3x the light intensity and take months to produce.

Equipment

Indoor setups need more gear than windowsill gardens. Here is the full list:

  • LED grow light panel or strip — The core investment. A full-spectrum white LED at 100–200 watts actual draw covers a 2x2-foot area.
  • Timer — Automates the light cycle. Inconsistent lighting triggers bolting.
  • Shelving unit — A 4-shelf wire unit (48" wide x 18" deep) is the most space-efficient setup. Each shelf becomes a growing level.
  • Containers — 4–6" pots for herbs, 10x20" trays for microgreens, 6–8" pots for lettuce. Use saucers underneath to contain water.
  • Potting mix — Indoor container mix. Skip added fertilizer for microgreen trays.
  • Small fan — Gentle airflow strengthens stems and prevents mold. Without it, plants go leggy and disease-prone.
  • Humidity tray — Pebbles and water in a shallow tray. Raises local humidity when indoor air drops below 30%.
Grow Light Guide

Lighting is the most important decision. Get this right and everything else is easy.

Greens need 150–250 PPFD; fruiting crops need 400–600+.

  • LED vs fluorescent. LEDs win. More light per watt, less heat, and 50,000+ hour lifespan vs 10,000 for fluorescents.
  • PAR and PPFD basics. PPFD measures usable light reaching your plants. Check manufacturer PPFD maps before buying.
  • Height from plants. Keep lights 12–18 inches above the canopy. Stretching means too far; bleaching means too close.
  • Photoperiod. Run lights 14–16 hours daily for greens and herbs. Never run 24 hours — plants need darkness.
  • Spectrum. Full-spectrum white LEDs (3500–5000K) work for all crops. Skip the purple "blurple" lights.
Step-by-Step Setup
  1. Choose a location. Any corner with an outlet works — spare room, closet, basement. Keep temperature at 60–75°F.
  2. Mount lights at proper height. Attach LED strips under each shelf, or hang a panel on adjustable chains. Start 14–16 inches above the canopy.
  3. Set the timer to 14–16 hours. Plug lights into the timer and leave it alone. Consistency prevents bolting.
  4. Arrange containers on trays. Waterproof trays catch drainage. Leave 1–2 inches between pots for airflow.
  5. Set up a fan. Gentle breeze across the canopy — leaves should barely rustle. Run it whenever lights are on.
  6. Monitor humidity. Target 40–60% with a digital hygrometer. Below 40%, add a humidity tray or humidifier.
Ventilation & Humidity

Plants transpire water through leaves. Without outdoor airflow, moisture builds up fast.

  • Mold risk. Stagnant humid air triggers damping off and powdery mildew. Both are preventable with airflow.
  • Fan placement. One small oscillating fan per shelf. Point it across the canopy, not at the soil.
  • Target humidity. 40–60% is ideal. Below 40%, add a pebble tray; above 60%, add ventilation.
  • Temperature interaction. Warm air holds more moisture. Keep temperature as consistent as possible between light-on and light-off periods.
Year-Round Schedule

Indoor gardens have no seasons. That changes your approach.

  • Stagger plantings. Start new batches every 1–2 weeks for an unbroken supply instead of feast-and-famine cycles.
  • Track planting dates. Label every container with date and variety. A phone note works fine.
  • Refresh soil regularly. Replace potting mix after 2–3 cycles. Use fresh mix for every microgreen tray.
  • Adjust for ambient changes. Room temperature and humidity shift seasonally even if lights stay constant. Check your hygrometer monthly.
Common Problems
Insufficient light intensity
Leggy, stretched growth and pale leaves mean not enough light. Lower lights to 12–14 inches above the canopy.
Overheating from lights too close
Bleached or brown leaf tips on topmost leaves. Raise the light 2–3 inches and monitor.
Humidity-related mold
White fuzz on soil or powdery mildew on leaves means excess humidity. Add a fan, space containers apart, and remove affected material.
Fungus gnats
Small flies around soil from overwatering. Let the top inch dry fully between waterings and use yellow sticky traps.
Leggy growth
Long thin stems with wide leaf spacing means light is too far, too dim, or on too few hours. Fix the light, then pinch stems to encourage branching.
