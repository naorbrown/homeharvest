Overview

A balcony is the first outdoor space most apartment dwellers can grow food in. Even a narrow 4-foot-wide balcony produces a surprising amount when you go vertical and pick the right containers.

Compared to a windowsill, you get more light, more airflow, and enough depth for tomatoes and peppers.

6+ hours of sun yields a steady supply of herbs, greens, cherry tomatoes, and peppers from spring through fall.

The tradeoffs: weather exposure, weight limits, and drainage. Solve those three and the growing itself is straightforward.

Best Crops

More light and deeper containers mean a wider crop range than a windowsill.

  • Herbs — All herbs thrive on balconies. Group basil, cilantro, parsley, rosemary, and thyme in railing planters.
  • Lettuce and greens — Loose-leaf lettuce, spinach, arugula, and chard do well in shallow containers. Give them morning sun and afternoon shade in midsummer.
  • Tomatoes — Cherry and bush varieties in 5-gallon pots with 6+ hours of direct sun. Stake or cage every plant.
  • Peppers — Sweet and hot peppers thrive in 3–5 gallon pots. South-facing balconies are ideal.

Avoid corn, melons, large squash, and full-size indeterminate tomatoes — they need more root space than containers offer.

Equipment

More gear than a windowsill setup because you are managing larger soil volumes, weather, and drainage.

  • Containers — 3–5 gallon pots for tomatoes/peppers, smaller troughs for herbs/greens. Match depth to roots: 6 inches for herbs, 12+ for tomatoes.
  • Railing planters — Hook over standard railings for herbs and small greens. Confirm your railing width and built-in drainage.
  • Saucers and drip trays — Non-negotiable. Put a large saucer under every pot and empty them regularly.
  • Potting mix — Use lightweight container mix with perlite. Avoid heavy garden soil.
  • Slow-release fertilizer — Mix granules in at planting for 3–4 months of feeding. Add liquid fertilizer every 2 weeks during fruiting.
  • Watering can or drip system — Narrow-spout can for control. Gravity-fed drip with a timer if you travel.
Step-by-Step Setup
  1. Assess sun exposure. Observe where direct sunlight hits throughout one full day. Sunniest spots go to tomatoes and peppers; shaded spots go to greens.
  2. Check weight limits. A full balcony garden can reach 200–400 pounds. Distribute weight along edges and near the wall, not in the center.
  3. Plan drainage. Water must not drip onto the balcony below. Use saucers under every container or switch to self-watering pots.
  4. Match containers to root depth. Herbs/lettuce: 6–8 inches. Peppers: 10–12 inches. Tomatoes: 12–14 inches.
  5. Add wind protection above floor 5. Place smaller pots in front of tall plants as a buffer. Shade cloth on the railing blocks gusts without blocking light.
Weight & Drainage

Critical for upper-floor balconies and often overlooked by new growers.

A 5-gallon pot of saturated potting mix weighs 30–40 pounds.

  • Saturated soil weight. Dry mix: ~0.5 lb/quart. Saturated: 1–1.5 lb/quart. Multiply by total containers to get your full load.
  • Use lightweight potting mix. Perlite and coconut coir mixes weigh far less than peat-heavy ones. Use fabric grow bags on weight-restricted balconies.
  • Manage runoff. Water slowly until it just drips from drainage holes, then stop. Empty saucers 30 minutes later.
  • Self-watering containers. Built-in reservoir wicks water up as soil dries. Reduces runoff to nearly zero.
Wind & Sun Exposure

Elevation changes everything. Balcony microclimates differ sharply from ground-level gardens.

  • Wind stress increases with height. Above floor 5, sustained winds snap stems and dry soil in hours. Anchor cages to pots or railings.
  • Walls amplify heat. Dark concrete or brick can push temps 10–20°F above ambient. Great for peppers; lethal for lettuce. If the pot is hot to touch, move it.
  • Orientation matters. South-facing is best. East works for greens/herbs. North-facing is tough — only lettuce and spinach will produce.
  • Overhangs reduce light. The balcony above may block overhead sun. Move containers to the railing edge for the widest angle.
Maintenance
  • Water daily in summer. Hot balcony containers can dry out in one day. Big tomato plants may need twice-daily watering at peak summer.
  • Feed regularly. Slow-release granules at planting plus liquid fertilizer every 2 weeks during fruiting. Yellow lower leaves usually mean nitrogen deficiency.
  • Prune and train. Sucker tomatoes, pinch first pepper flowers, and keep herbs trimmed for bushy growth.
  • Inspect for pests weekly. Check leaf undersides for aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites. A strong water spray dislodges most of them.
  • Rotate crops between seasons. Never plant tomatoes in the same pot two years running. Refresh at least the top half of the mix each season.
Common Problems
Wind damage
Stake all tall plants and anchor pots against the wall or railing. Group pots together for a windbreak effect.
Overheating against walls
Wilting with moist soil or leaf scorch on the wall side means heat stress. Move pots 6–12 inches from the wall or switch to light-colored containers.
Limited drainage options
Self-watering containers eliminate most runoff. With standard pots, water slowly and stop when the saucer fills.
Pest issues from neighboring plants
Aphids, spider mites, and whiteflies travel between balconies. Inspect weekly and isolate any heavily affected plant immediately.
