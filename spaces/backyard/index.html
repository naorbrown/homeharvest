Overview

A backyard garden is a different category from containers. Ground access means deeper roots, better moisture retention, and the ability to grow crops containers cannot support — root vegetables, squash, corn, and perennial fruit bushes.

A single 4x8-foot raised bed produces enough salad greens for a family all season.

The tradeoff is more upfront work: soil prep, sun planning, pest management, and seasonal maintenance. But yield per square foot is dramatically higher than any container setup.

The approach is always the same: choose the sunniest spot, build good soil, and start smaller than you think you need.

Best Crops

With ground access and full sun, you can grow everything. Prioritize highest return on space and effort:

  • Herbs — Plant near your kitchen door for daily harvest. Perennials (rosemary, thyme, oregano) go in permanent spots.
  • Lettuce and greens — Succession plant every 2–3 weeks for continuous harvests. Tuck into partial shade or gaps between slower crops.
  • Tomatoes — The most rewarding backyard crop. One plant yields 10–20 pounds per season.
  • Peppers — Produce heavily in warm soil with full sun. Compact plants that fit beds or edges.
  • Microgreens — Grow trays outdoors in mild weather for faster growth than indoor setups.
  • Root vegetables — Carrots, beets, radishes, and potatoes need ground depth containers cannot provide. Radishes mature in 25–30 days.
  • Squash and cucumbers — One zucchini plant is enough for a household. Train cucumbers vertically to save ground space.
Equipment

Backyard growing requires real tools. Here is the core set:

  • Raised bed materials — Cedar or untreated lumber for frames. Cedar resists rot and lasts 8–10 years.
  • Garden tools — Spade, steel rake, hand trowel, and wheelbarrow. Buy quality once — cheap tools break within a season.
  • Hose with adjustable nozzle — Multi-pattern nozzle covers most watering needs. For larger setups, soaker hoses deliver water directly to roots.
  • Compost bin — A three-sided bin from pallets or wire mesh works. A tumbler composter works for smaller spaces.
  • Row cover — Extends the season by 2–4 weeks on each end. Also protects against frost, insects, and birds.
  • Stakes and ties — Tomato cages, bamboo stakes, and twine for tall and vining crops. Budget more support than you think — loaded plants are heavy.
Step-by-Step Setup
  1. Choose the sunniest spot. Find the area with the longest uninterrupted direct sun. 6 hours minimum, 8+ ideal — south-facing with no overhead obstruction is best.
  2. Test your soil pH. Get a test kit or send a sample to your local extension service. Most vegetables need pH 6.0–7.0.
  3. Decide: raised beds or in-ground. Raised beds win if your soil is clay, rocky, or compacted. In-ground works if soil is already decent and you want lower startup cost.
  4. Build beds if needed. Standard size is 4x8 feet — reachable from both sides, productive, and manageable. Leave 2–3 feet of path between beds.
  5. Amend soil with compost. Mix 2–4 inches of compost into the top 8–10 inches. For new raised beds: 60% topsoil, 30% compost, 10% perlite.
  6. Tall crops go north. Tallest crops (tomatoes, corn) on the north edge so they do not shade shorter ones. Low crops (lettuce, herbs) on the south edge for full sun.
  7. Install simple irrigation. Soaker hoses or drip tape on a timer. 20–30 minutes every other day beats sporadic hand watering.
Raised Bed Construction

Building your first raised bed is straightforward:

  • Size. 4 feet wide by 8 feet long, 10–12 inches tall. Reachable from both sides without stepping in.
  • Materials. Cedar is best — rot-resistant and chemical-free, lasts 8–10 years. Never use pressure-treated lumber or railroad ties.
  • Construction. Use 2x10 or 2x12 lumber: two 8-foot and two 4-foot lengths. Screw corners with 3-inch deck screws, reinforce with metal brackets.
  • Bottom protection. Line with hardware cloth to block gophers and moles. Lay cardboard underneath to smother invasive grasses.
  • Filling. A 4x8x1-foot bed needs about 32 cubic feet of soil mix. Water thoroughly, let settle a few days, then top off before planting.
Composting

Composting turns kitchen and yard waste into free, high-quality soil amendment.

Compost is the single most valuable amendment you can produce for free.

  • Kitchen scrap collection. Keep a countertop bin for veggie scraps, peels, coffee grounds, and eggshells. Avoid meat, dairy, and oils — they attract pests.
  • Carbon to nitrogen ratio. 3:1 ratio of browns to greens by volume. Browns: dry leaves, cardboard, straw. Greens: kitchen scraps, grass clippings.
  • Turning schedule. Turn every 1–2 weeks with a pitchfork. A turned pile finishes in 2–3 months; unturned takes 6–12.
  • Finished compost signs. Dark brown, crumbly, smells like forest soil. If you can still identify ingredients, it needs more time.
  • Worm bin alternative. Red wiggler worms process scraps into rich castings indoors or in a garage. No odor when maintained properly.
Seed Saving

Saving seeds cuts cost and adapts crops to your specific conditions over time.

  • Save from your best plants. Choose the healthiest, most productive plants. Never save from the weakest performer.
  • Self-pollinating crops are easiest. Tomatoes, peppers, beans, peas, and lettuce produce true-to-parent seeds. Cross-pollinators (squash, corn) need isolation distances.
  • Dry thoroughly. Spread seeds in a single layer for 1–2 weeks. A properly dried seed snaps when bent.
  • Store cool and dark. Labeled envelopes in a sealed jar, refrigerator ideal. Add a silica gel packet for moisture.
  • Viability. Most seeds stay viable 2–5 years stored properly. Test old seeds: sprout 10 on a wet paper towel — if fewer than 6 germinate, buy fresh.
Seasonal Planning

A productive garden follows a seasonal calendar:

  • Late winter (6–8 weeks before last frost). Start tomato, pepper, and eggplant seeds indoors. Order seeds, clean tools, turn compost.
  • Early spring (2–4 weeks before last frost). Direct sow peas, spinach, radishes, lettuce, carrots. Add compost to beds and set up irrigation.
  • After last frost. Transplant tomatoes, peppers, squash outdoors. Mulch transplants with 2–3 inches of straw.
  • Midsummer. Succession plant greens every 2–3 weeks. Side-dress heavy feeders with compost.
  • Late summer (8–10 weeks before first frost). Plant fall crops: broccoli, kale, cabbage, beets, carrots. Flavor improves after light frost.
  • Fall. Harvest warm-season crops before frost. Plant garlic for next year. Mulch empty beds with leaves and compost.
Common Problems
Poor soil
The fix for clay and sand is the same: work 3–4 inches of compost into the top 8 inches annually. Within 2–3 years, even bad soil becomes productive.
Shade from trees and structures
Prune lower branches to raise the canopy. If shade is unavoidable, grow shade-tolerant crops: lettuce, spinach, kale, parsley.
Deer and rabbits
Only reliable deer deterrent: 8-foot fence. For rabbits: 2–3 foot chicken wire buried 6 inches deep.
Drainage issues
Standing water rots roots. Raised beds fix this instantly by elevating the root zone.
Soil compaction
Never step in planting areas — that is why 4-foot-wide beds work. If already compacted, add compost and aerate with a broadfork.
